Andromeda

The Chained Maiden

Key Facts

Located by tracing a line from the northeast corner of the Great Square of Pegasus to the northeast. This constellation is also mostly seen upside down and contains seven main stars.

  • Best viewed between the months of October and November.
  • Located in the center of th Autumn seasonal chart.
  • Alpheratz is the alpha star.

"This constellation notably contains the Andromeda galaxy (M31), a spiral-shaped deep sky galaxy similar to our own Milky Way. Andromeda galaxy is visible to the naked eye." [1, pg116]

Messier Objects

  • M31
  • M32
  • M110


IAU and Sky & Telescope magazine (Roger Sinnott & Rick Fienberg), CC BY 3.0, via Wikimedia Commons

Antila

The Air Pump

Key Facts

Located south of Leo, about five fist-widths from Regulus.

  • Best viewed in the months of March and April.
  • Located in the southwest quadrant of the Spring seasonal chart.
  • Alpha Antila is the alpha star.

"Northern Hemisphere stargazers can pick out this small constellation in the spring near the southern horizon. It contains two binary stars, one of which can be split with good binoculars." [1, pg117]



IAU and Sky & Telescope magazine (Roger Sinnott & Rick Fienberg), CC BY 3.0, via Wikimedia Commons

Aquarius

The Water Bearer

Key Facts

Located south of the Great Square of Pegasus.

  • Best viewed in the months of September and October.
  • Located in the southwest quadrant of the Autumn seasonal chart.
  • Sandalmelik is the alpha star.

"A constellation of the zodiac, Aquarius occupies a lot of celestial real estate along the ecliptic-the apparent path of the sun across the night sky-between Pisces and Capricornus." [1, pg118]

Messier Objects

  • M2
  • M72


IAU and Sky & Telescope magazine (Roger Sinnott & Rick Fienberg), CC BY 3.0, via Wikimedia Commons

Aquila

The Eagle

Key Facts

Forms part of the Summer Triangle asterism with Vega in Lyra and Deneb in Cygnus. It is located in an area of the Milky Way that has abundant star fields.

  • Best seen in the months of August and September.
  • Located in the southeast quadrant of the Summer seasonal chart.
  • Altair is the alpha star.

"Named by ancient Mesopotamian stargazers, Aquila the Eagle appears close enough to Earth's Equator to be seen from any terrestial viewing position." [1, pg119]



IAU and Sky & Telescope magazine (Roger Sinnott & Rick Fienberg), CC BY 3.0, via Wikimedia Commons

Aries

The Ram

Key Facts

A constellation of the Zodiac, it can be located between the Great Square of Pegasus and the Pleiades asterism in Taurus.

  • Best viewed in the months of November and December.
  • Located in the southwest quadrant of the Winter seasonal chart.
  • Hamal is the alpha star.

"Ancient astronomers traditionally placed Aries at the beginning of the celestial zodiac because, millennia ago, the sun was 'in' Aries at the time of the vernal equinox in spring." [1, pg120]



IAU and Sky & Telescope magazine (Roger Sinnott & Rick Fienberg), CC BY 3.0, via Wikimedia Commons

Auriga

The Charioteer

Key Facts

Located on a line between Orion and Polaris, it has seven main stars.

  • Best viewed in the months of December and January.
  • Located in the center of the Winter seasonal chart.
  • Capella is the alpha star.

"The tidy, elegant form of Auriga appears in the heart of the Milky Way and is on Ptolemy's list of the 48 original ancient constellations." [1, pg121]

Messier Objects

  • M36
  • M37
  • M38

Boötes

The Herdsman

Key Facts

This constellation contains eight key stars; one of which (beta star Nekkar) points towards the North Star (Polaris). Its alpha star lies along an arc from the handle of the Big Dipper. This gave rise to the mnemonic "arc to Arcturus."

  • Best viewed in the month of June.
  • Located in the center of the Summer seasonal chart.
  • Arcturus is the alpha star.

"The ancient constellation of Boötes is a celestial highlight of early summer. Its alpha star, Arcturus, ranks as fourth brightest star in the sky." [1, pg122]



IAU and Sky & Telescope magazine (Roger Sinnott & Rick Fienberg), CC BY 3.0, via Wikimedia Commons

Camelopardalis

The Giraffe

Key Facts

This constellation was created in 1613 to fill the celestial gap between Ursa Major, Ursa Minor, and Perseus. It contains five key stars, one of which (Z Camelopardalis) is a cataclysmic variable star that increases and decreases in brightness.

  • Best viewed in the months of December and January.
  • Located in the northeast quadrant of the Winter seasonal chart.
  • Alpha Camelopardalis is the alpha star.

"Camelopardalis is located in a region of the sky where few stars are visible. No star in this faint constellation has a common name." [1, pg123]



IAU and Sky & Telescope magazine (Roger Sinnott & Rick Fienberg), CC BY 3.0, via Wikimedia Commons

Cancer

The Crab

Key Facts

This Zodiac constellation is located between Leo and Gemini. There are five dim stars in this constellation as well as the Beehive Cluster (M44) that can be seen with the naked eye in dark skies.

  • Best viewed in the months of March and April.
  • Located in the southwest quadrant of the Spring seasonal chart.
  • Acubens is the alpha star.

"As a constellation of the zodiac, tiny Cancer the Crab is far less noticeable than most. It has no stars with a magnitude brighter than 4." [1, pg124]

Messier Objects

  • M44, Beehive Cluster, Praesepe
  • M67


IAU and Sky & Telescope magazine (Roger Sinnott & Rick Fienberg), CC BY 3.0, via Wikimedia Commons

Canes Venatici

The Hunting Dogs

Key Facts

This constellation is only two stars and is located right below the handle of the Big Dipper.

  • Best viewed in the months of May and June.
  • Located in the center of the Spring seasonal chart.
  • Cor Caroli is the alpha star.

"Location is everything in the night sky: The two stars of Canes Venatici appear just about where two leashed hounds would be expected in relation to Boötes, the Herdsman." [1, pg125]

Messier Objects

  • M3
  • M51, Whirlpool Galaxy
  • M63
  • M94

Canis Major

The Larger Dog

Key Facts

This constellation contains eight key stars. Follow Orion's belt southeast to find Sirius, the brightest star.

  • Best viewed in the months of January and February.
  • Located in the southeast quadrant of the Winter seasonal chart.
  • Sirius is the alpha star.

"Canis Major appears just to the southeast of the constellation Orion, not far from the Milky Way. The constellation's alpha star, Sirius, outshines all other stars in the night sky." [1, pg126]

Messier Objects

  • M41


IAU and Sky & Telescope magazine (Roger Sinnott & Rick Fienberg), CC BY 3.0, via Wikimedia Commons

Canis Minor

The Smaller Dog

Key Facts

The alpha star, Procyon, forms part of the Winter Triangle asterism. In early December this constellation is host to the Canis Minorids meteor shower.

  • Best viewed in the months of January and February.
  • Located in the southeast quandrant of the Winter seasonal chart.
  • Procyon is the alpha star.

"Orion's smaller hunting dog, Canis Minor, lies directly northeast of its larger companion, Canis Major. The dim constellation lack any bright deep sky objects." [1, pg127]



IAU and Sky & Telescope magazine (Roger Sinnott & Rick Fienberg), CC BY 3.0, via Wikimedia Commons

Capricornus

The Sea Goat

Key Facts

This constellation of the Zodiac contains twelve stars of low brightness, but their distinctive shape makes the constellation recognizable. It lies southeast of Aquila's alpha star Altair.

  • Best viewed in the months of August and September.
  • Located in the southeast quadrant of the Summer seasonal chart.

"The broad and distinctive triangle of Capricornus stands out in the southern sky, especially in late summer and early fall. Algedi, Arabic for 'the goat,' is the zodiacal constellation's alpha star." [1, pg128]

Messier Objects

  • M30


IAU and Sky & Telescope magazine (Roger Sinnott & Rick Fienberg), CC BY 3.0, via Wikimedia Commons

Cassiopeia

The Queen

Key Facts

The five stars of this constellation portray Queen Cassiopeia sitting on her throne looking to the north star (top of W / bottom of M are on the north side).

  • Best viewed in the months of October and November.
  • Located in the northeast quadrant of the Autumn seasonal chart.
  • Shedar is the alpha star.

"Visible year-round because of its proximity to the north celestial pole, Cassiopeia's iconic W shape (or M, depending on the season) makes identification easy." [1, pg129]

Messier Objects

  • M52
  • M103


IAU and Sky & Telescope magazine (Roger Sinnott & Rick Fienberg), CC BY 3.0, via Wikimedia Commons

Cepheus

The King

Key Facts

Find this constellation by first finding Cassiopeia and Ursa Minor and looking for the house shape between them. Depending on the year, it can be rightside up or upside down.

  • Best viewed in the mounths of September and October.
  • Located in the center of the Autumn seasonal chart.
  • Alderamin is the alpha star.

"A circumpolar constellation, Cepheus is visible all year in the Northern Hemishphere, but the brightness of its main stars competes with surrounding stars, often making it hard to spot." [1, pg130]
